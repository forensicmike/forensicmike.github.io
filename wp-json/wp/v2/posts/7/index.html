My first post ought to be about a subject I spent years working on. Little did I know what I expected to be a straightforward forensic examination of a wiped LG phone would result in so much — becoming a pivotal piece of evidence that contradicted the suspect’s alibi, my first real foray into reverse engineering Android APKs, the subject of several presentations, and even getting published in a technical op-ed in the INTERPOL Digital Forensics e-Newsletter.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

In recent times, location tracking data from iOS has come to the forefront with the work of people like Sarah Edwards (@iamevltwin<\/a>), Phill Moore (@phillmoore<\/a>) and Alexis Brignoni (@alexisbrignoni<\/a>). Turns out, LG’s tracking us too – except they’re trying to be sneaky by obfuscating the data that’s tracked with numeric column and table names. In reality, I think it’s likely that all <\/strong>the mobile vendors are doing this ‘diagnostic’ tracking and it’s just a matter of time (and forensic examinations) before that data gets uncovered. LG does give an explicit warning that this is going to happen regardless, but most people decline to actually read this stuff. <\/p>\n\n\n\n
